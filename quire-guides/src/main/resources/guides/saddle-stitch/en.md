## What is saddle stitch? {#overview}

Saddle stitch is one of the simplest and most widely used bookbinding techniques.
All pages are printed on sheets of paper that are folded in half and nested together
to form a single signature. The book is then stapled or sewn through the fold.

The result is a flat, clean booklet that opens easily. It is the binding you will
find in most magazines, brochures, and small catalogues.

![Overview of a saddle-stitched booklet](images/overview.png)

## When to use this technique {#when-to-use}

Saddle stitch works best for:

- Booklets of up to about 64 pages (16 sheets of paper)
- Documents where a flat, lightweight result is important
- Projects where simplicity and speed matter

For longer documents, consider **sewn signatures** instead, which distributes the
sewing load across multiple signatures and produces a more durable result.

## Tools and materials {#tools-materials}

You will need:

1. A **long-arm stapler** (for the stapled variant) — standard office staplers cannot
   reach the centre fold of an A4 sheet. A long-arm stapler extends to 30cm.
   Alternatively, use a Japanese screw punch and needle and thread for the sewn variant.
2. A **bone folder** for crisp, accurate folds.
3. A **ruler and pencil** for marking fold lines and staple positions.
4. A **cutting mat** to protect your work surface.
5. A **guillotine trimmer or scissors** for trimming the fore-edge if required.

For paper: 70–90 gsm text weight is ideal. Heavier paper produces more creep
(the outward shift of inner pages) and becomes difficult to fold and staple.

> **Tip:** For a professional result, use a slightly heavier card stock (200–300 gsm)
> for the cover sheet. This adds stiffness and protects the pages.

## Preparing your pages in Quire {#preparation}

Before printing, use Quire to prepare your PDF:

1. Open your PDF in Quire and select **Saddle stitch** as the binding technique.
2. Quire will show you the total page count. It must be a multiple of 4.
   If it is not, Quire will calculate how many filler pages to add and ask you
   where to place them.
3. Add any aesthetic pages (cover, endpapers) at the front and rear if desired.
4. Configure page numbering if needed — front matter in Roman numerals, body in Arabic.
5. Select your paper size and the **Folio** imposition layout.
6. Review the print preview to verify the page order looks correct.
7. Export the imposed PDF.

> **RTL note:** For right-to-left binding, select RTL in the reading direction setting.
> Quire will reverse the page order so the spine is on the right and pages read
> from right to left.

## Printing your sheets {#printing}

Print the imposed PDF on a duplex (double-sided) printer.

- Make sure **double-sided printing** is enabled and set to **flip on short edge**
  (also called "short edge binding" or "flip on short side" depending on your printer).
- Print a single test sheet first and fold it to verify the page order before printing
  the full document.

> **Warning:** The wrong duplex setting will produce a document where the back of each
> sheet is upside down. Always test with one sheet.

## Folding the signature {#folding}

1. Collect all printed sheets in order (page 1 on top if LTR; last page on top if RTL).

> **RTL note:** For RTL binding, the first sheet to fold is the one carrying the last
> pages of the document. Stack sheets with the highest page numbers on top.

2. Fold all sheets together in one action, aligning the edges carefully.
3. Use your bone folder to sharpen the fold, working from the centre outward toward
   the edges. This produces a clean spine and reduces wrinkling.
4. Check the page order by flicking through the folded booklet.

## Binding the book {#binding}

### Stapled variant

1. Open the folded booklet to the centre spread.
2. Mark two staple positions on the fold line: one 20mm from the top, one 20mm from
   the bottom.
3. Place the booklet on a flat surface, open at the centre spread, and staple through
   the fold at both marked positions.
4. Close the booklet and press the staples flat with your bone folder.

### Sewn variant

1. Open the folded booklet to the centre spread.
2. Mark 3–5 hole positions evenly spaced along the fold line. Begin and end 20mm from
   the top and bottom edges.
3. Use a Japanese screw punch or awl to pierce holes through all layers at each mark.
4. Thread a needle with waxed linen thread (length = 3× the spine height).
5. Begin at the centre hole, sewing outward to one end, then back to the centre, then
   to the other end, and back to the centre. Tie off the thread at the centre hole.

## Trimming the edges {#trimming}

The fore-edge (the open side of the booklet) may be uneven because the inner sheets
extend slightly further than the outer sheets (this is creep).

For a clean result, trim the fore-edge with a guillotine cutter:

1. Open the booklet to the centre spread and measure how far the innermost sheet
   extends beyond the cover.
2. Mark a trim line on the cover.
3. Trim with a single clean cut.

> **Tip:** If you are binding many copies, a stack trimmer produces the cleanest result.
> For one-off projects, a sharp craft knife against a metal ruler and cutting mat works well.

## Tips and common mistakes {#tips}

> **Tip:** Always fold with the grain direction. Paper folds more cleanly parallel to the
> grain. To find the grain, flex the paper gently in both directions — it resists more
> across the grain and bends more easily with it.

> **Tip:** For booklets over 32 pages, use thinner paper (70 gsm or lighter) to reduce
> creep and make folding easier.

> **Warning:** Do not use more than about 16 sheets (64 pages) in one saddle-stitched
> signature. The fold becomes very bulky and the staples or thread cannot hold securely.
> For longer documents, switch to sewn signatures binding.

> **Warning:** Standard office staplers cannot reach the centre fold of an A4 sheet.
> If you do not have a long-arm stapler, use the sewn variant instead.
